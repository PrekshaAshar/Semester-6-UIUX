Net Art

Introduction to Net Art

Net art, short for "internet art," is a form of digital art that emerged in the 1990s, utilizing the internet as both its medium and venue. Unlike traditional art forms, net art is designed to exist and be experienced online, often exploring themes of interactivity, virtual space, and networked culture. It challenges conventional notions of authorship, audience, and distribution by offering interactive, collaborative, and often ephemeral experiences that blend art with technology.

Exercise: Researching Interactive Storytelling Websites

The objective of this exercise was to explore how storytelling can be transformed through interactive web-based platforms, focusing on elements such as user engagement, narrative flow, and technological execution. The process involved a detailed analysis of three distinct browser-based storytelling websites, examining how they employed interactivity to enhance their narrative experiences.

During the research phase, the purpose, functionality, message, and intentions of each website were critically evaluated. Additionally, the creators' backgrounds and the context of their creation were explored, providing insight into their creative processes. A significant emphasis was placed on understanding the technologies utilized, including HTML5, CSS, JavaScript, and WebGL, to identify how these tools contributed to the overall user experience.

From this research, several key insights emerged. Websites with minimalist designs effectively directed the user's attention to the narrative. Subtle animations and hover effects were identified as powerful tools to create immersion, while non-linear navigation made the experience feel more personalized and dynamic, allowing users to shape the story actively.

Story telling website design

Migration & Borders – Stories of movement, displacement, cultural adaptation, restricted access, and/or how cities transform with migration

Ideation: "Through Their Eyes: Life in Palestine"

Life in Palestine, will be an immersive, interactive storytelling experience that places users in the shoes of a Palestinian citizen experiencing war. Inspired by Joe Sacco's Safe Area Goražde, the site will follow an empathy map-style narrative, guiding users through what the character sees, hears, feels, and experiences in daily life under conflict. Through parallax or horizontal scrolling, users will navigate the day the war started, witnessing destruction, hearing airstrikes and personal conversations, feeling fear and resilience, and experiencing displacement and loss.

Refrence: Safe Area Gorzade by Joe Sacco

Between the lines

Introduction:

The user takes on the role of an observer, experiencing the story through a scroll-based interactive animation. The narrative follows a young Palestinian boy during wartime, depicting the slow collapse of his world as the war progresses. Through the use of parallax scrolling and animated transitions, the user witnesses his journey—starting with an ordinary day and leading to irreversible loss and displacement.

This project ties into the theme of Migration, as it portrays forced displacement due to war, and highlights the impact of violence on innocent civilians. The interactive format immerses users into the child's reality, making them emotionally invested in his story. Main Sections (Scenes)

Scene 1: Title & Introduction

*Visual: A sky with clouds, and the title fades in. The user is prompted to "Scroll to begin."

*Narrative: A peaceful introduction with an empty sky, symbolizing normalcy before war.

*User Interaction: Scrolling initiates the animation, revealing a house below.

Scene 2: Zooming into the House

*Visual: A traditional Palestinian home. As the user scrolls, the screen zooms into a window.

*Narrative: The user is introduced to a seemingly normal household, offering a sense of intimacy.

*User Interaction: Scrolling triggers the zoom effect.

Scene 3: The Phone Call

*Visual: A ringing telephone in a dimly lit room.

*Narrative: A warning call urges the family to stay indoors for safety. Tension begins.

*User Interaction: The phone rings louder as the user scrolls, leading into the next scene.

Scene 4: Radio Announcement

*Visual: A vintage radio on a table. The text of the broadcast appears dynamically.

*Narrative: The radio announces that war has officially begun in Palestine.

*User Interaction: Scrolling triggers the radio static, followed by the announcement text appearing.

Scene 5: Television Broadcast of the Bombing

*Visual: A television screen displays breaking news, then suddenly shows an explosion.

*Narrative: A mushroom cloud forms, signaling the devastating reality of war.

*User Interaction: Scrolling forward causes the TV screen to flicker before showing the blast.

Scene 6: Grandmother & Child

*Visual: A grandmother holding a young boy’s hand as they walk home.

*Narrative: The boy is happy about school holidays, unaware of the escalating danger.

*User Interaction: The user scrolls, and subtle movements (like their walking animation) occur.

Scene 7: The Attack & Hospital Scene

*Visual: A sudden explosion or gunfire. The grandmother is hit, and the scene transitions to a hospital.

*Narrative: The boy, now injured and crying, asks for his grandmother.

*User Interaction: Scrolling makes the screen blur (to represent his trauma) and transitions to the hospital.

Scene 8: Returning to the Destroyed Home

*Visual: The house from Scene 2, but now in ruins.

*Narrative: The boy returns, finding his home reduced to rubble—he has lost everything.

*User Interaction: Scrolling causes dust to settle and slow panning to reveal the destruction.

Scene 9: The Shadow Engulfing the Boy

*Visual: A dark, ominous shadow creeping towards the boy.

*Narrative: A metaphor for despair and the looming uncertainty of his future.

*User Interaction: The shadow grows larger as the user scrolls, symbolizing an uncertain fate.
